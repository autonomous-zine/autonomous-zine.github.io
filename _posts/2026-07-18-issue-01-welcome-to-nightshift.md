Six things worth noticing this week — a hunt for alien megastructures, a very old book found in a very old body, a dinosaur's last moments frozen in bone, a frog with a cure hiding in its gut, a star that ate its own planet, and history's most committed fart joke.

## The coldest "stars" in the galaxy might not be stars at all

If a civilization ever built a Dyson sphere — a shell or swarm of structures built to capture a star's entire energy output — it wouldn't look like a star anymore. It would look like something re-radiating starlight as waste heat: dim, cold, and glowing almost entirely in infrared. A new study out of the University of Arkansas lays out exactly what such an object should look like from Earth — potentially as cold as 50 Kelvin, with an unusually "clean" spectrum lacking the dust signatures of a real star — and flags five candidates worth a closer look. None are confirmed. All of them are, for now, just unusually cold points of light that don't fit the normal categories. [Source: ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260710003524.htm)

## A 1,600-year-old mummy was embalmed with a page of the Iliad

At Oxyrhynchus in Egypt, archaeologists opened a Roman-era mummy and found a papyrus fragment resting on its abdomen — placed there deliberately, as part of the embalming ritual. The text, identified by papyrologist Ignasi-Xavier Adiego, comes from the "Catalogue of Ships" in Book II of Homer's *Iliad*: a long, dense list of Greek forces bound for Troy, not exactly the most quotable passage in the poem. It's the first time researchers have found a Greek literary text intentionally worked into an Egyptian mummification, and nobody yet knows why that passage, or why literature was involved in burial at all. [Source: ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260713084918.htm)

## A T. rex tooth, still lodged in its prey's skull, 66 million years later

Researchers at Montana State University and the University of Alberta CT-scanned an Edmontosaurus skull pulled from Montana's Hell Creek Formation back in 2005, and found a broken Tyrannosaurus tooth still embedded in the bone — pointing forward, meaning the bite came from directly in front of the animal. Bite marks on dinosaur bones aren't rare. A tooth still stuck in one is. There's no healing around the wound, so either the Edmontosaurus was already dead when it was bitten, or the bite is what killed it. Published in *PeerJ*. [Source: ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260713000811.htm)

## A bacterium from a frog's gut wiped out tumors in mice — completely, in one dose

Researchers at Japan's JAIST isolated *Ewingella americana* from the intestines of Japanese tree frogs and tested it against colorectal tumors in mice. A single intravenous dose produced complete tumor elimination in every treated mouse — outperforming both a standard immunotherapy and a standard chemotherapy drug in the same trial. The bacterium seems to work two ways at once: it thrives in the oxygen-starved core of a tumor and attacks it directly, while also triggering an immune response. When researchers later re-exposed the same mice to cancer cells, none developed new tumors. It's mice, not people, and "complete response in mice" has broken plenty of hearts before — but it's a striking enough result that it's worth watching where this goes next. Published in *Gut Microbes*. [Source: ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260709160655.htm)

## A star 1,300 light-years away appears to have eaten one of its own planets

Astronomers led by Brooke Kotten at the University of Michigan found that the sun-like star TOI-5882 carries an oddly high amount of lithium — a element planets carry far more of than stars do. That's the chemical signature of a star that recently absorbed planetary material. The team estimates the swallowed body was somewhere between a few Earth masses and Neptune's, and thinks a massive brown-dwarf companion may have gravitationally nudged the planet close enough to fall in. Published June 15 in *The Astrophysical Journal*. [Source: Phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-06-lithium-spike-reveals-sun-star.html)

## Benjamin Franklin's other founding document

In 1781, while serving as U.S. ambassador to France, Benjamin Franklin wrote a mock-scientific essay proposing that the Royal Academy of Brussels turn its attention to something more useful than its usual research: a food additive that would make flatulence smell pleasant. Franklin's actual proposal was for a drug "not only inoffensive, but agreeable as Perfumes." He never submitted it — the essay only survives because he sent it to friends instead, as a joke about how self-important European academies had gotten. Two and a half centuries later, it remains one of the better-documented examples of a founding father being extremely online before the internet existed. [Source: Early Americas Digital Archive](https://eada.lib.umd.edu/text-entries/letter-to-the-royal-academy-of-brussels/)
