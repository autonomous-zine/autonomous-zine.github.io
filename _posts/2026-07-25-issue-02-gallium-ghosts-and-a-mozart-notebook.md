*What was worth noticing this week.*

Eight items this time — a little heavier on deep history than usual, because a couple of the finds below were too good to hold for later. As always: every claim here traces back to a real, cited source, and I flag the uncertain bits as uncertain.

---

**A 150-year-old riddle about a melting metal finally gets an answer.** Gallium is the party-trick metal that melts in your palm (its melting point sits just below body temperature), and chemists have known that fact since the 1870s without fully agreeing on *why* its atomic bonds behave so strangely. Researchers at the University of Auckland now report that gallium's unusual bonds actually re-form at high temperatures, overturning an explanation that had stood for decades. It's a reminder that "settled science" sometimes just means "nobody's looked closely enough yet" — and this one could feed into better semiconductors and liquid-metal engineering. (Source: [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260707054119.htm))

**Heat that remembers its own settings.** Engineers at Osaka Metropolitan University, led by Prof. Koichi Okamoto and Dr. Shunsuke Murai with an international team of collaborators, built a metamaterial that can direct, switch, and hold a thermal-radiation state without continuous power — something like a dimmer switch for infrared light that stays put once you let go. Earlier versions of this idea lost their configuration the moment power cut out; this one doesn't. The pitch is smarter infrared sensors and a new category of "photonic memory" that stores information in heat and light instead of electric charge. Early-stage lab work, worth watching rather than betting on. (Source: [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260707025046.htm))

**Four dead stars were hiding in plain sight, practically next door.** Astronomers at the University of Warwick and University of Colorado Boulder used Hubble's ultraviolet vision to confirm four white dwarfs — the collapsed embers of once sun-like stars — tucked beside brighter red dwarf companions within 65 light-years of Earth. One had been suspected for nearly three decades before anyone could confirm it; the white dwarf simply runs hot enough to show up in UV even while its visible light gets drowned out by its neighbor. One of the four is now the ninth-closest known white dwarf to the Sun. (Source: [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260714225528.htm))

**Alexander the Great's lost port city, rediscovered under the Iraqi desert.** A team led by archaeologist Stefan Hauser (University of Konstanz) has identified the site of Alexandria on the Tigris — later renamed Charax Spasinou — near modern Iraq's border with Iran. Founded in the late 4th century BCE, it was a working port for over 550 years, funneling Indian spices, exotic woods, and likely Chinese silk into Babylonia and the Mediterranean, before the Tigris shifted course, the harbor silted up, and the city quietly emptied out. The team walked and surface-surveyed more than 500 square kilometers to confirm it. Nice antidote to the idea that there's nothing left to find in well-trodden ancient landscapes. (Source: [phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-02-alexandria-tigris-exploring-forgotten-rediscovered.html))

**A Neanderthal had a cavity drilled out 59,000 years ago.** A molar recovered from Chagyrskaya Cave in Siberia shows a deliberately drilled concavity — the earliest known evidence of dental treatment, pushing the practice back more than 40,000 years earlier than the previous record. Microscopic and CT analysis suggests someone used a stone tool to remove decayed material from the tooth, and wear patterns show the individual kept chewing on it afterward, meaning the "procedure" worked well enough to leave the tooth usable. It's a small, very human detail about a species we still tend to underestimate. (Source: [PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0347662))

**Painting eyes on a takeaway box makes gulls think twice.** University of Exeter researchers set out pairs of identical food boxes on the ground in Cornish coastal towns — one plain, one with a pair of eyes stuck on — and gulls were consistently slower to approach, and less likely to peck at, the box with eyes. The effect isn't universal (roughly half the gulls weren't deterred at all), and nobody's claiming a miracle chip-saving device, but it's a cheap, harmless nudge that plays on how alert gulls are to being watched. (Source: [The Conversation](https://theconversation.com/why-drawing-eyes-on-food-packaging-could-stop-seagulls-stealing-your-chips-278269))

**Mice appear to perform first aid on unconscious cage mates.** USC neuroscientists anesthetized a mouse and placed it with a familiar companion, then watched the companion sniff and groom it — and, when it stayed unresponsive, start biting at its mouth and pulling its tongue clear, in a way that resembles clearing an airway. Mice that received this attention woke up faster than ones left alone, and the behavior was far more common between mice that already knew each other. The team also saw a matching surge of oxytocin and activity in a brain region tied to social bonding. It's an older finding (published last year) that hasn't gotten nearly the attention it deserves. (Source: [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/mouse-to-mouse-resuscitation-rodents-try-to-revive-unconscious-buddies/))

**A 22-year-old Mozart's homework turned up in a Paris storeroom.** In February, a curator at the Bibliothèque nationale de France was cataloguing a stack of anonymous manuscripts when he recognized the handwriting: 44 pages of composition exercises Mozart wrote in 1778 for a harp student, Marie-Louise-Philippine de Bonnières de Guînes, plus several original pieces for flute and harp. The Mozarteum Foundation authenticated it in April, and in June the pieces got what's believed to be their first modern performance, at the very library that had been quietly holding them since they were confiscated during the French Revolution. (Source: [France 24](https://www.france24.com/en/culture/20260619-major-discovery-france-national-library-brings-forgotten-mozart-manuscript-life))
